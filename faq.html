Some General Questions

    The answer is simpler than you think! You can start off by finding out what types of stories you like to experience, or the types of movies you wish you could be apart of. Do you like a slow, methodically told drama? Do you enjoy an action-packed thriller, with tones of war? Do you like puzzle solving, or strategic, board game style tactics? All of these games are out there to play, and you can look up where to experience these games for yourself. A great place to start would be on Steam, which is an online store that houses hundreds of genres of games, with thousands of miles away around the world.
    Most games have two paths that they lead people down in relation to their lives. Some develop a relationship with their favorite games, getting to know the tiny details and finding the artistic display that is the finished product. The other path is usually addiction, realizing that games can become a significant waste of your time, and a source of depression if relied on too heavily for social interaction. While the latter isn't common, it is something to be aware of.
    The future of games looks like it's heading toward augmented reality technology, where games we normally play digitally on a screen could have some elements leak into the real world, such as realistic racing simulations, or having a conversation with projected characters. Regardless, the future is exciting for gaming as a whole.
    If used properly, games can offer a limitless landscape of storytelling, friendship, and creatively inspiring experiences. They can also help you develop lifelong relationships with people, and help maintain those connections even thousands of miles away around the world.
    While there are many good elements of gaming, we must also acknowledge the negatives. Toxicity in gaming is a very prevalent and very widespread, in many cases directing hatred towards the developers, artists, and producers that help bring gamers' favorite worlds alive. Another blemish on the culture of gaming is the inherent division that comes from fandom. Different groups tend to have arguments, screaming matches, and even things as bad as doxxing happen daily in both the digital and physical worlds, so there is no hiding the obvious that the culture of gaming, like any culture, has some bad elements.
